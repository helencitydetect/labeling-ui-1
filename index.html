Here's what the system does and why the mapping matters: What it is A labeler opens a photo of a house, picks checkboxes to mark what problems they see — cracked sidewalk, broken window, overgrown lawn, roof damage — and each checkbox saves a short code to a database. The codes like LABR, FES, wib are what actually get stored and used downstream. Who uses it Typically municipal governments, property analytics companies, or real estate data firms. They run thousands of photos through human labelers (or train AI models on the labeled data) to automatically detect code violations, assess property condition, or track neighborhood blight over time. Why the new simplified system The old system had 93 granular attributes — distinguishing between Mold, Paint rust, Structural damage separately for every single object. The new system collapses these into ~33 broader labels like Fence Damage or Roof Damage. This usually happens for one of these reasons: Training an AI model — fine-grained labels are hard to learn from photos. A model can't reliably distinguish "mold" from "paint rust" on a fence from street level, but it can learn "fence damage." Fewer, cleaner classes = better model accuracy. Labeler reliability — human labelers disagree a lot on subtle distinctions like mold vs structural. Merging them reduces inter-labeler variance and makes the dataset more consistent. Cost — fewer decisions per image = faster labeling = cheaper at scale. What actually matters downstream — a city inspector doesn't need to know if it's specifically mold or paint rust on a shutter; they just need to know the shutter is in bad shape and send someone to look. What's being lost and why it matters The 33 removed attributes — especially Mold everywhere, all of Shutter, Railing, Vent, Soffit, Gutter — means those specific defect types will no longer be tracked individually. If anyone downstream was querying "how many properties have vent mold" or "railing structural damage," that data simply stops being collected.
